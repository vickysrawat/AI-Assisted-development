AI-Assisted Development · A Field Report

What's left for us?

The AI writes the code now. The interesting question was never whether it replaces us — it's what's left. I keep coming back to three things it can't take: craft, responsibility, and team. Here's what each one means now.

VR
Vivek Rawat
On developing in the age of AI · ~9 min read

Every few weeks, someone on my team asks the quiet version of the question out loud: if the AI writes the code, what's left for me? For a while I fumbled the answer — some reassuring noise about "higher-level work." I don't fumble it anymore, because I've watched closely what actually changed, and what didn't.

For most of my career the deal was simple. I understood the change, I made it, I pushed it, and I told the lead: review it, ping me if there's a problem. That worked — but not because the process was clever. It worked because the peer on the other end was human. A human pushes back. A human asks "are you sure?" A human notices when you're confidently heading in the wrong direction.

Your peer is now an AI. It's fast, it's fluent, and it will follow you off a cliff without blinking. The loop still runs — make it, push it, review it — but the one step that quietly checked intent is gone. Nobody is asking "are you sure?" anymore. The first time I really saw that, I stopped worrying about being replaced and started worrying about something more useful: everything the AI can't do is now, unambiguously, my job.

I stopped worrying about being replaced, and started worrying about everything the AI can't do — because that's now unambiguously my job.

So — what's left for us? Three things. Not vague things. Three specific parts of the work the AI genuinely cannot take. Craft. Responsibility. Team. Let me take them one at a time.

I. Craft

Knowing what "right" means here

The AI has never once asked me what "good" looks like on my codebase. It can't. That question — and the taste to answer it — is craft, and it didn't go anywhere.

For years I thought craft lived in the typing. The elegant function, the clever reduction, the diff that made a reviewer nod. So when the typing became free, I braced for craft to feel cheap. It didn't. It moved.

Because the AI gives you plausible, and plausible is a trap. It compiles, it reads well, it looks like something a competent person wrote — and it can still be quietly, expensively wrong for your situation. It doesn't know that this is one abstraction too many. It doesn't know that this name will confuse the next person in six months, or that the "clever" solution becomes a liability the moment traffic doubles, or that in this domain that shortcut is the difference between a warning and a lawsuit. It knows the average of everything ever written. You know where you actually are.

That's the part that stayed with us: taste, and the judgment to know what "right" means here. Right isn't universal — it's local. It lives in the context you carry and the standards you hold, and neither of those fits in a prompt. When the machine can generate ten plausible answers in a second, the scarce, valuable thing is the person who can look at all ten and say "none of these — here's why," and be right.

There's an uncomfortable edge to this, and I'd rather say it out loud. When the mechanical work disappears, there's nowhere left to hide. You used to be able to look busy and productive shipping volume. Now the volume is free, and the only thing that distinguishes you is judgment. That's a gift to anyone who spent years building taste — and a reckoning for anyone who mistook typing speed for skill. Craft used to be something you had; now it's something you have to deliberately keep sharpening, because it's suddenly the whole game.

The AI gives you plausible. Plausible is a trap. "Right" is local — and knowing what right means here is the craft that never left.

Craft is the first thing that's left, and honestly the biggest. But taste, on its own, is just opinion — until someone's name is on the outcome. Which is the second thing.

II. Responsibility

It never left

When the feature breaks in production at 2am, no one pages the model.

This is the part I find genuinely reassuring, and it took me a while to see why. Everything about how we work changed. This didn't move an inch: accountability never transferred. The AI has no skin in the game. It doesn't carry the pager. It doesn't feel the Monday-morning post-mortem, doesn't lose sleep over the customer who got burned, doesn't wear the outage on its reputation. You do. That asymmetry is the whole relationship — and it's permanent.

There's a quiet temptation now to launder responsibility through the machine. The AI wrote it. I've caught myself reaching for it. But it doesn't hold, and it never will: "the AI wrote it" is just the new "the compiler generated it" — true, and completely beside the point. You framed the problem, you chose that answer over the alternatives, you approved it, you shipped it. The moment it left your hands with your sign-off on it, it became yours. Authorship was never the thing you were accountable for. Judgment and consequence were.

If anything, responsibility got heavier. You're now answerable for code you didn't type — output you delegated the making of but not the owning of. That's a new discipline, and it's harder than it sounds: you have to read the machine's work as critically as if you'd written it yourself, because as far as your team, your users, and your name are concerned, you did. The developer who rubber-stamps a confident diff they didn't really understand isn't saving time. They're the one holding the pager for a decision they never actually made.

And it comes with an obligation to be honest — about what you know and, more importantly, what you don't. Owning an outcome means not hiding behind the tool, flagging the parts you couldn't verify, and signing off on evidence, not vibes. That's not process for its own sake. That's just what it has always meant to be a professional.

"The AI wrote it" is the new "the compiler did it" — true, and beside the point. You shipped it. It's yours.

So responsibility is the second thing that's left — and notice what it really means: the thing that makes you a professional, being answerable, is exactly the thing that can't be delegated. That's not a small consolation. That's your whole value, made undeniable. But you're not carrying it alone anymore — because there's a new colleague in the room. Which is the third thing.

III. Team

We don't just use the new colleague— we lead them

We hired someone brilliant, tireless, and a little overconfident. They started this morning, they'll have forgotten everything by tomorrow, and they're already writing production code. Leading them is the job now.

Here's the reframe that changed how I work: stop calling it a tool. A tool waits to be picked up. This thing proposes, drafts, decides-ish, and acts — it behaves far less like a hammer and far more like a person on your team. A very fast, very junior, strangely uneven person, but a teammate all the same. And you don't use a teammate. You lead them.

Leading is a real skill, and most of us were never taught it until we made senior — if we ever did. The strange gift of this moment is that it arrives on day one now. A developer two years into their career is suddenly directing a report: framing the work, handing over context, judging what comes back. The people who thrive in this won't be the fastest typists. They'll be the ones who can set a direction and hold a standard.

And leading this particular colleague looks a lot like leading any junior, just compressed. You give clear intent instead of a vague ask. You hand over enough context that the work comes back right the first time. You state the standard you expect, and you notice the instant the work drifts from it. You catch the confident mistake before it ships. And you teach — because this colleague has one strange flaw: they forget everything overnight. So part of leading them is refusing to let hard-won knowledge evaporate, holding the team's memory so the same lesson isn't relearned every single morning. Good leads have always done that for their people. Now it's literal.

One more thing that didn't disappear: the human team. The PM sharpening the intent, the QA engineer who thinks in failure modes, the peer who reviews your reasoning and not just your syntax — they're all still here, and coordinating them is still the work. The AI joined the team. It didn't replace it. Leading now means conducting both: the humans, and the tireless new hire who never sleeps.

You don't use a teammate. This thing behaves like a very fast, very junior colleague — so the job quietly became leading them.

What's left

So — what's left for us? Craft, responsibility, and team. The judgment to know what "right" means here. The ownership that was always ours and never moved. And the leadership to direct a new kind of colleague alongside the old ones.

Notice what those three have in common: none of them was ever really about the typing. We just spent so much of our days typing that we mistook it for the job. The AI didn't take the good part of the work. It took the part that was keeping us from it.

That's the reframe I'd offer anyone feeling the ground move right now. What's left isn't the scraps. It's the part we always said mattered most and rarely had the time to do well. So the next time someone on your team asks the quiet question — if the AI writes the code, what's left for me? — you can tell them the truth:

Everything that was ever actually the point.
